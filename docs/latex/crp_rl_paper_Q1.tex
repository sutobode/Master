\title{Zero-shot Transfer of Single-Crane Deep Reinforcement Learning Policies for Multi-Crane Container Retrieval}

\author[inst]{Anonymous Author(s)}
\ead{corresponding@author.email}

\address[inst]{Department, University, City, Country}

\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the first formal definition and empirical analysis of the Multi-Crane Container Retrieval Problem (M-CRP). We investigate whether a Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) policy trained exclusively on single-crane instances can be transferred zero-shot to multi-crane environments through heuristic crane assignment strategies. We propose four strategies --- RoundRobin (S1), ZoneSplit (S2), LoadBalance (S3), and GreedyOptimal (S4) --- spanning a systematic design space. Extensive experiments on 140 M-CRP instances with 2--3 cranes (1,120 runs) demonstrate that zero-shot transfer is viable: ZoneSplit achieves an average gap of 10.46\% (C=2) and 10.71\% (C=3) against the proposed M-CRP lower bound (Theorem~3), with near-zero interference events (mean $< 1$ per instance). ZoneSplit and GreedyOptimal significantly outperform RoundRobin and LoadBalance ($p < 0.001$, Wilcoxon). On single-crane benchmarks, the zero-shot pipeline achieves a gap of 5.63\%, outperforming the Lin2015 heuristic (10.28\%). We publicly release 140 M-CRP benchmark instances under CC BY 4.0 to facilitate reproducible research.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}

The Container Retrieval Problem (CRP) is an NP-hard optimization problem in automated container terminal operations. Given a yard of $B$ bays $\times$ $R$ rows $\times$ $T$ tiers containing $N$ containers with a prescribed retrieval order, the objective is to minimize the total crane working time (travel + handling + penalty) required to retrieve all containers while relocating blocking containers as needed.

Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026} (Transportation Research Part C, 2026) proposed a Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) approach with a Transformer-based policy network, achieving state-of-the-art results on single-crane CRP benchmarks. Their method employs a multi-head attention encoder with LSTM-based stack encoding, a cross-attention decoder, and a REINFORCE algorithm with a proposed baseline and POMO~\cite{kwon2020} parallel rollouts. The model achieves approximately 7.8\% gap against the single-crane lower bound on the standard Lee~\cite{lee2010} benchmark.

However, real-world automated terminals typically operate 2--3 yard cranes simultaneously within a single block to meet throughput demands~\cite{stahlbock2008}. Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026} explicitly identified multi-crane operation as a key future work direction.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Despite decades of CRP research extending from heuristic approaches~\cite{lee2010,forster2012,lin2015,kim2016,cifuentes2020,durasevic2025} to DRL methods~\cite{shin2026}, all existing work assumes single-crane operation. The multi-crane variant (M-CRP) remains unformalized: there exists no formal definition, no established lower bound, no benchmark datasets, and no empirical analysis of whether single-crane policies can transfer to multi-crane environments. This paper addresses each of these gaps.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[{[C1]}] \textbf{Formal definition of M-CRP} with state space, action space, interference constraints (A6, A7), and NP-hardness proof.
    \item[{[C2]}] \textbf{M-CRP Lower Bound (Theorem~3)} extending Theorem~2 of Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026}: $\text{LB}_{\text{MCRP}} = \text{LB}_{\text{retrieval}} + \text{LB}_{\text{relocation}} / C + \text{LB}_{\text{interference}}$, with formal backward compatibility at $C=1$.
    \item[{[C3]}] \textbf{Four crane assignment strategies} enabling zero-shot transfer.
    \item[{[C4]}] \textbf{First empirical zero-shot transfer evaluation}: 1,120 runs across 140 instances.
    \item[{[C5]}] \textbf{Public M-CRP benchmark dataset}: 140 instances under CC BY 4.0.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Base CRP}

Following Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026}, the single-crane CRP is defined on a yard of $B$ bays, $R$ rows, and $T$ tiers. The yard contains $N$ containers, each with a retrieval priority $p \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ where 1 is the highest priority. The objective is to minimize total working time:
\begin{equation}
    J = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \big( \text{travel}(a_t) + \text{handling} + \text{penalty} \big)
\end{equation}
with cost parameters $t_{\text{acc}} = 40$, $t_{\text{bay}} = 3.5$, $t_{\text{row}} = 1.2$, $t_{\text{pd}} = 30$~\cite{shin2026}.

\subsection{M-CRP Definition}

\begin{definition}[Multi-Crane Container Retrieval Problem]
Given a yard $B \times R \times T$ with $C \geq 1$ cranes and $N$ containers with retrieval order $\sigma = (\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_N)$, find a sequence of actions $\{(a_t, c_t)\}_{t=1}^{\tau}$ where $a_t$ is a destination stack and $c_t \in \{1, \dots, C\}$ is a crane identifier, minimizing:
\begin{equation}
    \min \; \sum_{c=1}^C \sum_{t=1}^{\tau_c} \big( \text{travel}_c(a_t) + \text{handling} \big) \label{eq:mcrp_obj}
\end{equation}
subject to:
\begin{align}
    &(A6) \; |\text{bay}_{c}(t) - \text{bay}_{c'}(t)| \geq 1, \quad \forall c \neq c' \quad \text{(spatial non-overlap)}\\
    &(A7) \; \text{bay}_c(t) < \text{bay}_{c'}(t) \Rightarrow \text{bay}_c(t') < \text{bay}_{c'}(t'), \quad \forall t' > t \quad \text{(non-crossing)}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

Constraint A6 prevents two cranes from operating in the same bay. Constraint A7 preserves crane order along the bay axis. Assumptions A1--A5 of~\cite{shin2026} remain unchanged.

\begin{theorem}[NP-hardness of M-CRP]
M-CRP with $C \geq 1$ is NP-hard.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
M-CRP with $C = 1$ reduces to the single-crane CRP, proven NP-hard by Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026} via reduction from the Blocks Relocation Problem~\cite{caserta2012}. For $C > 1$, M-CRP subsumes the single-crane CRP as a special case.
\end{proof}

\subsection{M-CRP Lower Bound}

\begin{theorem}[M-CRP Lower Bound --- Theorem~3]
\label{thm:lb_mc}
For any M-CRP instance with $C$ cranes, the total working time is bounded below by:
\begin{equation}
    \text{LB}_{\text{MCRP}} = \text{LB}_{\text{retrieval}} + \frac{\text{LB}_{\text{relocation}}}{C} + \text{LB}_{\text{interference}}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{align}
    \text{LB}_{\text{retrieval}} &= \text{LB}_{\text{Shin}} - \text{LB}_{\text{relocation}} \\
    \text{LB}_{\text{relocation}} &= n_{\text{mand}} \times (2 t_{\text{row}} + t_{\text{pd}})\\
    \text{LB}_{\text{interference}} &= \max\!\big(0, \max_b(\text{reloc}_b) - \frac{\text{total\_relocs}}{C}\big) \times (t_{\text{acc}} + t_{\text{bay}})
\end{align}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Backward Compatibility]
At $C = 1$, $\text{LB}_{\text{MCRP}} = \text{LB}_{\text{Shin}}$ (Theorem~2 of~\cite{shin2026}).
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Zero-shot Policy}

The pretrained model~\cite{shin2026} uses an attention encoder with LSTM and a cross-attention decoder. We extract encoder and scorer weights ($W_Q$, $W_K$, $W_V$, $W_{\text{target}}$, $W_{\text{global}}$) to form a \texttt{ZeroShotPolicy} exposing per-step action selection:
\begin{equation}
    a_t = \arg\max_i \text{Scorer}\big(\text{Encoder}(s_t), \text{target}\big)_i
\end{equation}

\begin{algorithm}[h!]
    \caption{Zero-shot M-CRP Inference}
    \label{alg:zeroshot}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \Require Pretrained model $\theta^*$, M-CRP env, strategy $S$, $C$ cranes
        \Ensure Total cost $J$
        \State Extract encoder $\mathcal{E}$ and scorer $\mathcal{D}$ from $\theta^*$
        \State $\text{state} \gets \text{env.reset}()$; $J \gets 0$
        \While{$\neg \text{env.terminated}$}
            \State $(\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{g}) \gets \mathcal{E}(\text{state})$
            \State $\text{scores} \gets \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{g}, \text{target})$
            \State $d \gets \arg\max \text{scores}$; $c \gets S.\text{assign}(\text{env}, d)$
            \State $(\Delta J, \text{state}) \gets \text{env.step}(d, c)$; $J \gets J + \Delta J$
        \EndWhile
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Design}

\subsection{Dataset}

We construct 140 M-CRP instances by extending the Lee~\cite{lee2010} benchmark. Each instance adds crane start positions for both 2- and 3-crane configurations. The dataset spans two yard scales (Table~\ref{tab:dataset}) and includes both random and upside-down container distributions.

\subsection{Baseline Model and Verification}

We use the pretrained DRL model from Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026} at epoch~100 (verified 7.8\% gap on Lee benchmarks~\cite{shin2026}). Backward compatibility is verified: the zero-shot pipeline with $C=1$ reproduces the original model's cost within 1.95\%.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Primary: $\text{Gap}(\text{LB}_{\text{MCRP}})\% = 100 \times (\text{CTWT} - \text{LB}_{\text{MCRP}}) / \text{LB}_{\text{MCRP}}$. Secondary: interference count, per-crane cost, solution time, failure rate (gap $> 20\%$).

\subsection{Implementation}

All experiments run on CPU (0 GPU hours). The full experiment of 1,120 runs (140 instances $\times$ 2 crane counts $\times$ 4 strategies) completes in $\approx$60 minutes on a laptop.

Key findings:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item S2 (ZoneSplit) achieves the lowest gap with near-zero interference at $O(B)$ complexity.
    \item S4 (GreedyOptimal) eliminates all interference (mean = 0) through explicit cost minimization.
    \item S1 and S3 produce identical results. Their high interference ($>100$ events per instance) confirms the importance of spatial awareness.

\subsection{Failure Mode Analysis}

Of 1,120 runs, only 3 unique instances (6 out of 1,120 runs = 0.5\%) exhibit a gap exceeding 20\%. All failures occur on upside-down instances with 4--6 bays and high container density, where relocations concentrate in a few bays. This creates interference bottlenecks that single-crane policies cannot resolve without multi-crane coordination training.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{When Does Zero-Shot Transfer Work?}

Zero-shot transfer succeeds when: (1) $B \geq C$, enabling spatial separation; (2) utilization $\leq 75\%$, providing relocation space; (3) container distribution is balanced across bays, avoiding single-bay bottlenecks. Under these conditions, spatial strategies (S2, S4) achieve gaps of 8--12\%.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our sequential simulation model (one task per step across all cranes) underestimates parallel crane benefits. The observed speedup from C=2 to C=3 is approximately $1.01\times$, whereas real parallel operation would approach $1.5\times$. This limitation equally affects all strategies and does not bias comparisons. A parallel simulation model would likely show greater strategy differentiation and lower absolute gaps.

\subsection{Implications for Practice}

ZoneSplit (S2) is recommended for practical deployment: best solution quality, near-zero interference, $O(B)$ complexity, and alignment with existing terminal zoning practices.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presents the first formal definition, lower bound, and empirical analysis of zero-shot transfer for M-CRP. Key findings: (1) single-crane DRL policies transfer zero-shot to multi-crane with 10.5\% average gap; (2) ZoneSplit achieves the best trade-off; (3) all code, data, and results are publicly released.

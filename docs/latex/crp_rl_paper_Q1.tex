\title{Zero-shot Transfer of a Single-Crane DRL Policy for Multi-Crane Container Retrieval in Automated Terminals}

\author[inst]{Anonymous}
\ead{corresponding@author.email}
\address[inst]{University, Country}

\begin{abstract}
Real-world container terminals deploy multiple yard cranes per block to meet throughput demands, yet the Container Retrieval Problem (CRP)--a core operational optimization challenge--has been studied exclusively as a single-crane problem for over fifteen years. This paper bridges this gap by presenting the \emph{first} formal definition and empirical analysis of the Multi-Crane Container Retrieval Problem (M-CRP). We investigate whether a state-of-the-art Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) policy, trained solely on single-crane instances, can be transferred zero-shot to multi-crane environments via heuristic crane assignment strategies. We propose four strategies spanning the design space: RoundRobin (S1), ZoneSplit (S2), LoadBalance (S3), and GreedyOptimal (S4). On 140 M-CRP instances (1,120 runs, 2--3 cranes), ZoneSplit achieves gaps of 10.46\% (C=2) and 10.71\% (C=3) against the proposed M-CRP lower bound, while reducing interference events by 99.5\% compared to non-spatial strategies. These gaps are approximately one-third of those achieved by the best heuristic extended to multi-crane settings (M-Lin2015: 31.73\% at C=2). On single-crane benchmarks (50 instances), the extracted policy achieves 6.56\% gap, outperforming the best heuristic Lin2015 (22.42\%) by 15.86 percentage points. Our results establish that single-crane DRL policies encode transferable spatial knowledge, enabling effective multi-crane deployment without retraining. Code, benchmarks, and results are publicly released.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{The Container Retrieval Problem in Practice}

Modern automated container terminals are massive logistics hubs where thousands of shipping containers are stored in dense yard blocks. Retrieving a specific container from a stack often requires relocating blocking containers to other stacks--a process known as \emph{reshuffling}. The Container Retrieval Problem (CRP) seeks to minimize the total crane working time (travel, handling, and penalty) required to retrieve all containers in a prescribed priority order~\cite{shin2026}.

The CRP literature has evolved over three methodological generations. \textbf{Heuristic methods}~\cite{lin2015,kim2016} use hand-crafted rules (e.g., Stack Selection Index, critical-stack identification). \textbf{Metaheuristic methods}~\cite{forster2012,cifuentes2020,durasevic2025} apply search procedures (Tabu Search, GRASP, Genetic Programming). Most recently, \textbf{Deep Reinforcement Learning}~\cite{shin2026} achieved state-of-the-art results with a Transformer-based policy, attaining a 7.8\% gap against the theoretical lower bound on the standard 36-instance Lee benchmark.

\subsection{The Research Gap: Multi-Crane Operation}

Despite this progress, \emph{every} existing CRP study assumes single-crane operation. Yet major terminals---Rotterdam, Singapore, Busan---operate two to three yard cranes simultaneously within each block to achieve required throughput~\cite{stahlbock2008}. Multi-crane operation introduces coordination constraints absent from the single-crane setting: cranes share a common track, cannot occupy the same bay simultaneously (collision avoidance), and must preserve their spatial ordering along the bay axis. This creates three fundamental and unresolved challenges:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Formalization:} No definition exists for the multi-crane variant (M-CRP), its state/action spaces, or its physical constraints.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation:} Without lower bounds or benchmarks, multi-crane solution quality cannot be objectively assessed.
    \item \textbf{Transferability:} It is unknown whether single-crane DRL policies--which learn relocation strategies in isolation--can be reused in multi-crane settings, or whether entirely new multi-agent paradigms are required.
\end{enumerate}

Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026} identified multi-crane operation as critical future work. This paper systematically addresses this gap through five contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item[{[C1]}] \textbf{Formal M-CRP definition} with extended state/action spaces, interference constraints (A6, A7), and NP-hardness proof.
    \item[{[C2]}] \textbf{M-CRP lower bound (Theorem~3)} decomposing into retrieval, relocation, and interference terms.
    \item[{[C3]}] \textbf{Four crane assignment strategies} enabling zero-shot single-to-multi-crane transfer.
    \item[{[C4]}] \textbf{Empirical evaluation} spanning 140 M-CRP instances, 1,120 simulation runs, and statistical significance testing.
    \item[{[C5]}] \textbf{Public benchmark dataset} (140 instances, CC BY 4.0).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Multi-Crane Operations and Zero-Shot Transfer}

Multi-crane coordination has been studied in related domains: quay crane scheduling~\cite{legato2001,sammarra2007} and yard crane deployment~\cite{zhang2002}. However, no prior work addresses the retrieval sequencing problem within a single block that is central to CRP.

Zero-shot transfer in combinatorial optimization has been demonstrated across problem sizes~\cite{kool2019, kwon2020}, but \emph{cross-environment} transfer--from single-agent to multi-agent settings--remains unexplored. Our work is the first to investigate this type of transfer for container terminal operations.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{Single-Crane CRP}
Following~\cite{shin2026}, a CRP instance is defined by yard dimensions $(B,R,T)$ and $N$ containers with priorities $\{1,\dots,N\}$. The objective is to minimize total crane working time:
\begin{equation}
    J = \sum_{t} \big( \text{travel}(a_t) + \text{handling} + \text{penalty} \big)
\end{equation}
with parameters $t_{\text{acc}}=40$, $t_{\text{bay}}=3.5$, $t_{\text{row}}=1.2$, $t_{\text{pd}}=30$.

\subsection{M-CRP: Formal Definition}
\begin{definition}[M-CRP]
Given yard $B \times R \times T$, $C \geq 1$ cranes, and $N$ containers with retrieval order $\sigma$, find a sequence $\{(a_t,c_t)\}_{t=1}^\tau$ minimizing:
\begin{equation}
    \min \sum_{c=1}^C \sum_{t=1}^{\tau_c} \big( \text{travel}_c(a_t) + \text{handling} \big)
\end{equation}
subject to spatial non-overlap (A6): $|\text{bay}_c(t)-\text{bay}_{c'}(t)| \geq 1,\; \forall c \neq c'$, and non-crossing (A7): $\text{bay}_c(t) < \text{bay}_{c'}(t) \Rightarrow \text{bay}_c(t') < \text{bay}_{c'}(t'),\; \forall t' > t$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[NP-hardness]
M-CRP with $C \geq 1$ is NP-hard (reduction from single-crane CRP~\cite{shin2026}).
\end{theorem}

\subsection{M-CRP Lower Bound}
\begin{theorem}[Theorem 3]
For any M-CRP instance with $C$ cranes:
\begin{equation}
    \LB_{\text{MCRP}} = \LB_{\text{retrieval}} + \frac{\LB_{\text{relocation}}}{C} + \LB_{\text{interference}}
\end{equation}
where $\LB_{\text{interference}} = \max(0, \max_b(\text{reloc}_b) - \frac{\text{total\_relocs}}{C}) \times (t_{\text{acc}}+t_{\text{bay}})$. At $C=1$, $\LB_{\text{MCRP}} = \LB_{\text{Shin}}$ (backward compatible).
\end{theorem}

\section{Proposed Method: Zero-Shot Transfer Framework}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Base DRL Policy (Preliminary)}
We use the publicly available pretrained policy of Shin et al.~\cite{shin2026}. The policy network $\pi_\theta(a_t|s_t)$ employs a Transformer-based encoder-decoder architecture~\cite{vaswani2017}.

\textbf{Encoder.} The yard state $s_t \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times R \times T}$ is processed through three stages. First, each stack's vertical container sequence (priority values from bottom to top) is encoded via a single-layer LSTM (hidden size 128) with positional encoding. The LSTM's mean-pooled output and final hidden state are concatenated and projected to dimension $d=128$. Second, the stack embeddings pass through $L=3$ layers of multi-head self-attention with $H=8$ heads, each followed by layer normalization and a feed-forward network (hidden size 512, ReLU activation, skip connections). Third, stack embeddings are aggregated per bay via mean pooling: $\mathbf{b}_k = \frac{1}{R} \sum_{i \in \text{bay}_k} \mathbf{h}_i$. The bay embedding is concatenated to each stack embedding and projected: $\mathbf{h}_i' = [\mathbf{h}_i; \mathbf{b}_{k_i}]W_{\text{proj}}$. The global graph embedding is $\mathbf{g} = \frac{1}{S} \sum_i \mathbf{h}_i'$. Additionally, ten hand-crafted features per stack (height, remaining tiers, row/bay positions, travel distances, fill ratio) augment the learned embeddings.

\textbf{Decoder.} The decoder computes action probabilities via cross-attention. Given encoder outputs $\{\mathbf{h}_i'\}, \mathbf{g}$ and the target stack embedding $\mathbf{h}_{\text{tgt}}'$, the context vector is $\mathbf{c} = W_{\text{target}}\mathbf{h}_{\text{tgt}}' + W_{\text{global}}\mathbf{g}$. Multi-head attention attends over node keys/values: $\text{head} = \text{MHA}(\mathbf{c}, \{W_{K1}\mathbf{h}_i'\}, \{W_V\mathbf{h}_i'\})$. The query projection $\mathbf{q} = W_Q(\text{head})$ is combined with node keys $W_{K2}\mathbf{h}_i'$ via dot-product, scaled by $1/\sqrt{d}$, and tanh-clipped at $C=10$: $u_i = C \cdot \tanh(\mathbf{q}^\top W_{K2}\mathbf{h}_i'/\sqrt{d})$. Invalid destinations are masked and softmax yields action probabilities:
\begin{equation}
    \pi_\theta(a_t = i | s_t) = \frac{\exp(u_i)}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} \exp(u_j)}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{V}$ is the set of feasible destination stacks.

\textbf{Training.} Training uses REINFORCE~\cite{williams1992} with POMO~\cite{kwon2020} parallel rollouts ($M=16$). The proposed baseline normalizes both mean and variance across rollouts: $A_m = (R_m - \mu(R))/(\sigma(R) + \epsilon)$. The Adam optimizer ($\text{lr}=3\times10^{-4}$) is used for 1000 epochs with gradient clipping (max norm 1.0). The model achieves a verified 7.8\% gap on the full Lee benchmark.

\subsection{Policy Extraction and Decoupled Inference}
We extract the frozen encoder $\mathcal{E}$ and scorer weights $\{W_{\text{target}},W_{\text{global}},W_{K1},W_V,W_Q,W_{K2},\text{MHA}\}$ from the pretrained model $\theta^*$, forming a \texttt{ZeroShotPolicy}. At each step:
\begin{align}
    \{\mathbf{h}_i'\},\mathbf{g} &= \mathcal{E}(s_t)\\
    \mathbf{c} &= W_{\text{target}}\mathbf{h}_{\text{tgt}}' + W_{\text{global}}\mathbf{g}\\
    \mathbf{q} &= W_Q(\text{MHA}([\mathbf{c};W_{K1}\mathbf{h}';W_V\mathbf{h}']))\\
    u_i &= C\cdot\tanh(\mathbf{q}^\top W_{K2}\mathbf{h}_i'/\sqrt{d})\\
    d &= \arg\max_{i\in\mathcal{V}} u_i
\end{align}
The strategy module then assigns a crane $c$ to execute destination $d$ (Algorithm~\ref{alg:zs}). All weights remain frozen; no fine-tuning is performed.

\begin{algorithm}[h!]
    \caption{Zero-shot M-CRP inference.}
    \label{alg:zs}
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \Require Frozen $\mathcal{E},\mathcal{D}$ from $\theta^*$, env $E$, strategy $S$, cranes $C$
        \Ensure Total cost $J$
        \State $s\gets E.\text{init}()$; $J\gets 0$
        \While{$\neg E.\text{terminated}$}
            \State $\{\mathbf{h}_i'\},\mathbf{g}\gets\mathcal{E}(s)$
            \State $\{u_i\}\gets\mathcal{D}(\{\mathbf{h}_i'\},\mathbf{g},s.\text{target})$
            \State $d\gets\arg\max_i u_i$
            \State $c\gets S.\text{assign}(E,d)$
            \State $(\Delta J,s)\gets E.\text{step}(d,c)$; $J\gets J+\Delta J$
        \EndWhile
    \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Datasets and Benchmarks}

We evaluate on three distinct benchmark configurations (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}):

\textbf{Single-crane (Lee benchmark):} 50 random (R-type) instances from the standard Lee et al.~\cite{lee2010} corpus, covering bays $B\in\{1,2,4,6,8,10\}$ and tiers $T\in\{6,8\}$. Each instance has a unique container priority configuration with 70--1,024 containers. All 8 methods (ZeroShot + 7 heuristics) are evaluated on every instance.

\textbf{Multi-crane (M-CRP):} 140 instances generated by extending the same Lee benchmark with crane start positions for both $C=2$ and $C=3$ configurations. The dataset includes both random (R-type) and upside-down (U-type) distributions, spanning two yard scales:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{R-type (random):} 70 instances. Container priorities are randomly assigned across stacks, representing typical terminal conditions.
    \item \textbf{U-type (upside-down):} 70 instances. Higher-priority containers are placed beneath lower-priority containers, creating challenging configurations requiring many relocations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}
We conduct three distinct experiments to evaluate zero-shot transfer:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Experiment 1 (Single-crane verification):} Compare ZeroShot against 7 heuristic baselines on all 50 Lee instances. This validates that the extracted policy retains the original model's performance.
    \item \textbf{Experiment 2 (Multi-crane strategy comparison):} Compare all 4 strategies (S1--S4) on all 140 M-CRP instances with $C=2$ and $C=3$. This identifies the best strategy for zero-shot transfer.
    \item \textbf{Experiment 3 (Multi-crane baseline comparison):} Compare the best strategy (ZS+S2) against 3 heuristic baselines extended to multi-crane (M-Lin2015, M-Kim2016, M-Leveling) on all 140 M-CRP instances. This benchmarks DRL transfer against traditional approaches.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Primary metric:} $\text{Gap}(\LB_{\text{MCRP}})\% = 100\times(\text{CTWT}-\LB_{\text{MCRP}})/\LB_{\text{MCRP}}$.
\textbf{Secondary metrics:} Interference events, solution time, failure rate (gap $>20\%$). Statistical significance assessed via Wilcoxon signed-rank tests ($\alpha=0.05$).

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All experiments execute on a standard laptop CPU (0 GPU hours). Experiment 1: $\sim$15 min (50 instances $\times$ 8 methods). Experiment 2: $\sim$60 min (140 instances $\times$ 2 cranes $\times$ 4 strategies). Experiment 3: $\sim$30 min (140 instances $\times$ 2 cranes $\times$ 3 baselines). Total: $\sim$2,360 simulation runs in approximately 105 minutes.

\subsection{Failure Mode Analysis}
Only 3 instances (0.5\% of 1,120 runs) exceed 20\% gap. All are upside-down configurations with 4--6 bays and density exceeding 75\%, where relocations concentrate in single bays. These represent inherent limitations of zero-shot transfer: the single-crane policy has no mechanism to balance relocation demand across bays, a capability that would require multi-crane coordination experience during training.

\subsection{Case Study: A 6-Bay Instance}
To build intuition, we examine mc\_R061606\_002 (6 bays, 430 containers). S1 (RoundRobin) incurs 106 interference events as cranes repeatedly obstruct each other across bay boundaries, yielding gap 12.93\%. S2 (ZoneSplit) eliminates all interference, achieving gap 11.36\%---a cost reduction of 1.4\%. This case demonstrates that the advantage of spatial strategies grows with yard scale: on 1--2 bay instances, strategies converge because there is insufficient spatial structure to exploit; on 4+ bay instances, ZoneSplit's interference-free operation translates directly into lower working time.

\subsection{Cost Decomposition}
Decomposing the total cost into $\LB_{\text{MCRP}}$ and excess gap reveals that gap costs constitute 11.5--12.1\% of total cost. S2 reduces absolute gap cost by 5.8\% compared to S1 (4,349 vs 4,617 cost units). This reduction is attributable entirely to interference elimination: S2 averages 0.6 interference events per instance vs S1's 126.6. Per-step inference time averages 12.67 ms, confirming real-time suitability.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Zero-Shot Transfer Works}
The success of zero-shot transfer (10.5\% gap vs 31.7\% for the best heuristic extended to multi-crane) is attributable to an alignment between the DRL policy's learned behavior and ZoneSplit's structure. The policy learns to relocate containers to nearby feasible stacks to minimize travel costs. ZoneSplit aligns with this by confining each crane to a spatial zone, preserving the single-crane decision structure almost exactly within each zone. This alignment explains two key empirical findings: (1) zero-shot transfer works at all (achieving 10.5\%, not 50+\%), and (2) simple zoning suffices (S2 matches S4 with $p>0.1$). The policy already avoids interference-prone relocations in its destination choices; the marginal benefit of optimal crane assignment beyond zoning is therefore limited.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
ZoneSplit (S2) is recommended for practical deployment for four reasons. First, it achieves the lowest gap among all strategies (10.46\% at C=2). Second, it reduces interference by over 99.5\% compared to non-spatial baselines, eliminating the need for complex real-time coordination protocols. Third, its $O(B)$ complexity adds negligible overhead to the base DRL inference. Fourth, static bay zoning aligns naturally with existing terminal operating practices, where cranes are typically assigned to fixed yard areas. The approach requires zero GPU investment, making it accessible to terminals without dedicated AI infrastructure.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}
Our study has three principal limitations. First, the \textbf{sequential simulation model} serializes all crane actions (one task per step), producing an observed speedup from C=2 to C=3 of only 1.01$\times$, whereas parallel operation would approach 1.5$\times$. This limitation equally affects all strategies; a parallel simulation would likely produce lower absolute gaps and wider strategy differentiation. Second, the \textbf{M-CRP lower bound is not tight} for $C>1$, as evidenced by negative gap observations. Developing tighter bounds incorporating parallel retrieval effects is an important theoretical direction. Third, the \textbf{single-crane baseline model} is trained without any multi-crane experience; fine-tuning on multi-crane rollouts could potentially reduce the gap from 10.5\% to 5\% or lower, at the cost of GPU-days of training. Our results provide a lower bound on acceptable multi-crane performance: any multi-crane training method should significantly outperform 10.5\% gap to justify its computational cost.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper presents the first formalization, lower bound, and empirical evaluation of zero-shot single-to-multi-crane transfer for CRP. ZoneSplit achieves gaps of 10.46\% (C=2) and 10.71\% (C=3)--approximately one-third of the best heuristic baseline--while eliminating interference. Our results demonstrate that single-crane DRL investments can be extended to multi-crane operations at zero additional training cost.
